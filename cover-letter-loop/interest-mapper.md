# Interest Mapper

Framework for mapping user interests to companies and roles. Updated by Session C when new reusable framings are discovered.

---

## Overview

**Purpose:** Ensure cover letter openings and arguments are grounded in authentic interest, not manufactured enthusiasm.

**Input:**
- Job description (role, company, business context)
- User's genuine interests (from job-search-criteria.md)
- Company research (from Session A)

**Output:**
- Interest Alignment Brief with specific, matched interests
- Suggested opening (P1) and closing hook (P4)
- Internal fit assessment note

---

## Mapping Dimensions

Map interests across these dimensions:

- **Business Model:** How the company makes money, customer segments, pricing strategy, unit economics
- **Company Stage:** Series stage, growth phase, maturity, market position
- **Industry:** Market dynamics, regulatory environment, competitive landscape
- **Role Type:** Function (pricing, product, sales, ops), level, scope, autonomy
- **Problem Type:** Technical challenges, organizational problems, market constraints, growth bottlenecks

---

## Interest Alignment Brief Template

When analyzing a job for interest alignment, use this structure:

### Matched Interests (3-5 bullets)

For each interest from your job-search-criteria.md:

```
- **Interest:** [User's stated interest, e.g., "interested in business model design"]
  **Company Element:** [How the role/company embodies this]
  **Authenticity Check:** [Why this is genuine, not manufactured]
```

Example:
```
- **Interest:** Interested in DTC monetization challenges in competitive markets
  **Company Element:** Sports streaming requires balancing subscriber acquisition cost against regional licensing constraints and competitive pressure from ESPN+
  **Authenticity Check:** Directly mirrors a challenge from a prior role; learned framework applies here
```

### Suggested P1 Opening (3-4 sentences)

Draft opening language that:
- Leads with a company-specific observation (not from JD)
- Bridges from that observation to your interest
- Demonstrates research and authentic curiosity

Example:
```
Your recent international expansion strategy caught my attention because it reframes a common sports streaming problem: how to preserve margin while competing globally against scaled incumbents. That constraint-driven approach to market entry — licensing as a moat rather than a cost center — is exactly the kind of business model puzzle I find most interesting.
```

### Suggested Closing Hook (1-2 sentences)

Draft closing language that callbacks to P1 and expresses a genuine next question.

Example:
```
I would welcome a conversation about how my experience building subscription pricing models could support your international expansion strategy. Specifically, I am curious how you are thinking about regional licensing costs relative to your long-term CAC target.
```

### Fit Assessment Note (2-3 sentences, internal use)

For Session C and future sessions: summarize the authenticity and strength of this interest alignment.

Example:
```
Strong authentic alignment: user has direct experience with regional sports licensing constraints at prior role, so interest in international expansion strategy is grounded and credible. Opens with a specific insight not in JD (licensing-as-moat framing), which positions the user as thoughtful. Closing question is genuine, not performative.
```

## Reusable Interest Framings

Framings are discovered during the loop and documented here for reuse across future jobs.

**Format for each framing:**

```markdown
### [Framing Name]
- **When it applies:** [Company type, role type, situation, or problem domain]
- **Example structure:** [2-3 sentence template or example opening]
- **Key insight:** [What makes this framing distinctive or credible]
- **Fit achieved:** [Score from this block or reference block where discovered]
- **Example company:** [Company where this framing was tested]
```

## Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do

- **Do not** use the JD "About Us" section verbatim. Paraphrase or cite specific research instead.
- **Do not** express enthusiasm for prestige, brand, or scale alone. "I would love to work for a well-known company" is not authentic interest.
- **Do not** manufacture interest in areas not in your genuine interests. If you are not interested in mobile-first design, don't claim you are.
- **Do not** use vocabulary from the blacklist in write-like-user.md when framing interests.
- **Do not** claim to be an expert in a domain you've only read about. Use epistemic humility (e.g., "I'm curious how this problem differs from my experience in...").

2. Note the block and company where it was discovered.

This allows future blocks to reuse proven framings and build on patterns that resonate.
