## Draft Structure

### Header
```
[Full Name] | [Email] | [Phone] | [City, State]
```

### Opening Line
Date and salutation:
```
[Date]

[Salutation — e.g., "Dear [Hiring Manager Name]," or "Dear Hiring Team,"]
```

### Body: Four Paragraphs

**P1 (Interest/Opening):** ~50 words
- Company-specific detail (not derivable from JD alone)
- Demonstrates genuine interest, not industry proclamations
- Sets up the argument

**P2 (Argument 1):** ~75 words
- One clear argument thread with specific evidence
- Thesis, proof, closer

**P3 (Argument 2):** ~75 words
- Second argument thread
- Same structure as P2

**P4 (Closing):** ~50 words
- Callback to P1 with fresh detail
- Forward-looking without presumption
- Specific ask or natural next step

### Closing
```
Sincerely,

[Full Name]
```

## Body Paragraph Rules

### Thesis-First Rule
Every paragraph opens with a sentence naming the capability or insight, not describing what you did.

**Bad:** "At Company A, I led a team that redesigned the pricing model."
**Good:** "I have led successful business model redesigns, most directly at Company A where I..."

### Through-Line Rule
One clear through-line per paragraph. Max 2 distinct proof points.

**Through-line:** A single idea (one capability, one insight) that threads through the paragraph.

If you want to include 3+ proof points, subordinate or cut one. Don't dilute the paragraph with multiple unrelated points.

**Bad:**
"I have diverse experience. At Company A, I built a pricing model. At Company B, I managed a team. At Company C, I negotiated partnerships."
(Four different points; no through-line.)

**Good:**
"I have built pricing models in both B2B and B2C contexts. At Company A, I redesigned our enterprise tier (resulting in 20% ARR growth). At Company B, I adapted the consumer model when we pivoted upstream."
(Through-line: pricing model design across contexts.)

### Proof-Then-Test Closer
Name the system you built/managed, name the condition that tested it, name the evidence.

**Structure:** "[System/capability] [condition/pressure] [evidence]."

**Example:** "My pricing automation framework was pressure-tested during a seasonal demand spike in Q2, handling 10x normal transaction volume without errors."

**Bad closer:** "I gained valuable experience." (No proof.)

## P1 (Opening) Rules

### Company-Specific Detail Requirement
Must include a company detail not derivable from the JD alone.

**Bad:** "I am excited to join a company focused on sports streaming."
(Derived from JD "We are a sports streaming platform.")

**Good:** "Your recent expansion into international markets, particularly the UK, caught my attention given the unique challenges of regional sports licensing."
(Not obvious from JD; shows research.)

### Epistemic Humility
Do not position yourself as an industry expert unless you genuinely are.

**Bad:** "As a sports streaming expert, I understand your market challenges..."
(Overclaiming unless you've actually worked in sports streaming.)

**Good:** "I've built pricing and retention strategies in subscription services, and I'm curious how those patterns differ in regional sports, where licensing constraints reshape your CAC calculation."
(Acknowledges limit of knowledge while claiming relevant experience.)

### Depth Over Breadth
One specific observation beats five vague claims.

**Bad:**
"I'm interested in your company because it's innovative, customer-focused, and has a great team."
(Generic; not specific.)

**Good:**
"Your strategy to unbundle regional sports packages (rather than bundling, like most competitors) is fascinating because it likely improves margin and unit economics by letting cost-conscious fans pick only the sports they care about."
(Specific observation; shows thinking.)

### No ATS Keyword Optimization in Opening
Do not stuff keywords from the JD. Write naturally.

**Bad:**
"As an experienced pricing analyst with proven DTC marketing expertise and cross-functional partnership experience, I am excited to apply my skills in business operations and strategic finance to your open role."
(Sounds like keyword matching, not writing.)

**Good:**
"Your business has a unique challenge: you're competing with national streaming giants (ESPN+, Amazon Prime Video) while tied to regional sports rights. That's a fundamentally different problem than national aggregation, and it's the kind of constraint-driven strategy I find most interesting."
(Authentic, specific, no keyword stuffing.)

## Closing Rules

### Callback with Fresh Detail
Refer back to the opening observation, but add a new angle.

**Bad:**
"Thank you for considering my application. I am excited about this role."
(Generic; no callback.)

**Good:**
"I'd welcome the chance to discuss how my experience scaling subscription margins could specifically help as you expand internationally. I'm genuinely curious how you're thinking about regional licensing as a cost center vs. a moat."
(Callback to opening research + fresh angle + specific ask.)

### Forward-Looking Without Presumption
Avoid presuming you'll get the job.

**Bad:**
"I look forward to joining your team and making an immediate impact."
(Presumptuous.)

**Good:**
"I'd welcome a conversation about how my analytics background could support your pricing strategy. Let me know if my background looks like a fit."
(Hopeful but grounded.)

### Specific Ask or Natural Next Step
Name what you want the next step to be.

**Examples:**
- "I would welcome a conversation with you or [Hiring Manager Name] about how my pricing background could support this role."
- "If my experience with subscription retention resonates, I'd be glad to discuss specific strategies we might deploy."
- "I would be happy to walk through a recent pricing migration I led and how the lessons apply here."
